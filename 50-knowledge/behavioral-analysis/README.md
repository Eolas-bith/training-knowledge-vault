# Behavioral Analysis

## Purpose

Reference material for applying behavioral science frameworks to intelligence analysis. These methods are used when the subject of an investigation is a human actor — a threat actor, a person of interest in an influence operation, a source being evaluated, or a decision-maker being profiled.

These frameworks are tools for generating hypotheses and structuring analysis, not for generating definitive psychological diagnoses. Every behavioral inference requires an explicit confidence level and an evidence basis.

---

## Seven Analytical Traditions

This section organises behavioral analysis methods across seven traditions, each with distinct assumptions, tools, and appropriate use cases.

| Tradition | Core Question | Primary Use Case |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Competitive Intelligence and Behavioral (CIB) | What will this actor do next? | Threat actor decision modeling |
| Psycholinguistics | What does this text reveal about the author? | Message analysis, attribution |
| Forensic behavioral analysis | What does the behavior pattern indicate? | Incident reconstruction |
| Threat assessment | What is the risk this actor poses? | Escalation prediction |
| Nudge / behavioral economics | What incentives drive this actor? | Influence operations, social engineering |
| Psychographic profiling | What are the actor's stable traits and values? | Long-term actor modeling |
| Social network analysis | How is behavior shaped by network position? | Influence mapping, key node identification |

## Principles

**Never diagnose.** Behavioral analysis produces hypotheses, not clinical diagnoses. The distinction matters analytically and legally.

**Confidence is mandatory.** Every behavioral inference must carry an explicit confidence level (HIGH / MODERATE / LOW) with a stated evidence basis. Behavioral inferences are inherently more uncertain than technical IOC analysis.

**Distinguish observation from inference.** "The message was sent at 2 AM local time" is an observation. "The author is under operational pressure" is an inference. Keep them separate.

**Context is evidence.** Behavior that seems anomalous in isolation may be normal in context. Always reconstruct the context before interpreting the behavior.

**Multiple hypotheses.** For any behavioral pattern, generate at least two competing explanations before settling on one. Use ACH or structured red-teaming to evaluate them.
